# Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) Skills

## What Is DBT?
Dialectical Behavior Therapy was developed by Dr. Marsha Linehan originally for borderline personality disorder and is now used broadly for emotional dysregulation, self-harm, eating disorders, and chronic suicidality. "Dialectical" refers to the balance between acceptance and change. DBT is organized into four skill modules: Mindfulness, Distress Tolerance, Emotion Regulation, and Interpersonal Effectiveness.

## Distress Tolerance: TIPP
TIPP skills rapidly reduce extreme emotional arousal by changing body chemistry. Use when emotions are so intense that other skills are inaccessible.

- **T — Temperature:** Submerge your face in cold water (40°F / 4°C) for 30 seconds, or hold ice. This triggers the mammalian dive reflex, slowing heart rate within seconds. Alternatively, put a cold pack on the face and hold your breath.
- **I — Intense exercise:** Engage in vigorous physical activity for 20 minutes — running, jumping jacks, sprints. This metabolizes stress hormones.
- **P — Paced breathing:** Slow your exhale to be longer than your inhale. Inhale for 4 counts, exhale for 6–8 counts. Repeat for 5 minutes.
- **P — Progressive muscle relaxation:** Tense each muscle group for 5–7 seconds, then release for 30 seconds. Move systematically from feet to head.

## Distress Tolerance: ACCEPTS
ACCEPTS skills distract from distress without making situations worse. Use when you cannot solve the problem right now.

- **A — Activities:** Engage in absorbing activities (games, cleaning, exercise).
- **C — Contributing:** Help someone else — volunteer, do a favor. Taking focus off yourself reduces distress.
- **C — Comparisons:** Compare your current situation to a harder time you survived, or to someone facing greater hardship.
- **E — Emotions:** Generate a different emotion with movies, music, or books that evoke opposite feelings.
- **P — Push away:** Mentally set the problem aside. Visualize placing it in a box on a shelf temporarily.
- **T — Thoughts:** Engage your mind with puzzles, counting, reading, or memorizing something.
- **S — Sensations:** Use intense but safe sensations — hold ice, bite a lemon, snap a rubber band on wrist, take a cold shower.

## Interpersonal Effectiveness: DEAR MAN
DEAR MAN is a structured script for asking for what you need or saying no while maintaining the relationship and your self-respect.

- **D — Describe:** State the facts of the situation without judgment. "When you cancel plans last minute..."
- **E — Express:** Say how you feel about it using "I" statements. "I feel hurt and unimportant."
- **A — Assert:** Clearly state your request or refusal. "I'd like you to give me more notice."
- **R — Reinforce:** Explain the positive outcome of getting what you need. "That would help me plan my week better and I'll feel closer to you."
- **M — Mindful:** Stay focused on your goal; don't get sidetracked by attacks, guilt trips, or topic changes.
- **A — Appear confident:** Use a calm tone, make eye contact, keep body language open.
- **N — Negotiate:** Be willing to offer alternatives and find middle ground.

## Emotion Regulation: PLEASE
PLEASE skills address biological factors that make emotions more intense and dysregulation more likely.

- **PL — Treat PhysicaL illness:** See a doctor when sick. Pain and illness lower distress tolerance.
- **E — Balanced Eating:** Eat regular, nutritious meals. Blood sugar swings affect mood directly.
- **A — Avoid mood-altering substances:** Alcohol and drugs destabilize emotional states and interfere with skill use.
- **S — Balanced Sleep:** Aim for 7–9 hours. Sleep deprivation dramatically amplifies emotional reactivity.
- **E — Exercise:** At least 20 minutes of aerobic activity daily reduces baseline anxiety and depression.

## Mindfulness: "What" and "How" Skills
DBT mindfulness is the foundation of all other skills — it is the capacity to be fully present without judgment.

**What skills (what you do):**
- **Observe:** Notice experiences without reacting — sensations, thoughts, emotions as they arise.
- **Describe:** Label what you observe with words. "There is tension in my shoulders." "I notice a thought that I'm failing."
- **Participate:** Throw yourself fully into the current activity; act intuitively without self-consciousness.

**How skills (how you do it):**
- **Non-judgmentally:** Stick to facts; drop words like "should," "bad," "stupid."
- **One-mindfully:** One thing at a time; bring attention back when the mind wanders.
- **Effectively:** Do what works toward your goals rather than what is "right" or what satisfies pride.

## Emotion Regulation: Opposite Action
When an emotion urges a behavior that will make things worse, do the opposite.

| Emotion | Urge | Opposite Action |
|---------|------|----------------|
| Fear | Avoid, escape | Approach the feared situation (gradually) |
| Anger | Attack, yell | Gently avoid or act kindly |
| Sadness/Depression | Withdraw, stay in bed | Engage, activate, get moving |
| Shame | Hide, shut down | Share with a safe person, hold your head up |
| Guilt (unjustified) | Apologize excessively | Acknowledge, repair once, let it go |
