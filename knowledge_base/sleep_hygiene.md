# Sleep Hygiene and CBT for Insomnia (CBT-I)

## Why Sleep Matters for Mental Health
Sleep and mental health are bidirectionally linked. Poor sleep worsens anxiety, depression, emotional dysregulation, and cognitive function. Conversely, treating insomnia often improves mood and anxiety symptoms significantly. The American College of Physicians recommends CBT for Insomnia (CBT-I) as the first-line treatment for chronic insomnia — ahead of sleep medications.

CBT-I targets the thoughts and behaviors that perpetuate insomnia, producing improvements that last beyond the treatment period (unlike medications, which lose effectiveness when discontinued).

## CBT-I Core Components

### 1. Sleep Restriction Therapy
Sleep restriction is the most powerful component of CBT-I. It consolidates fragmented sleep by temporarily limiting time in bed to match actual sleep time, creating mild sleep pressure.

**Steps:**
1. Track sleep for one week using a sleep diary. Record: time in bed (TIB), estimated total sleep time (TST), number of awakenings.
2. Calculate your baseline sleep efficiency (see formula below).
3. Set a new sleep window equal to your average TST (but never less than 5 hours). For example, if you sleep 5.5 hours on average, your window is 5.5 hours.
4. Choose a fixed wake time (e.g., 6:30 AM) and count backward to set your bedtime (e.g., 1:00 AM).
5. Stay out of bed until your new bedtime. Get up at your fixed wake time no matter what.
6. Each week, if sleep efficiency exceeds 85%, extend the sleep window by 15–30 minutes (earlier bedtime).

**Expected experience:** The first 1–2 weeks are difficult — you will feel sleepier. This is the mechanism working. Mild daytime sleepiness indicates you are building the sleep pressure required to consolidate sleep.

### 2. Sleep Efficiency Formula
Sleep efficiency is the primary metric in CBT-I.

**Formula:**
```
Sleep Efficiency (%) = (Total Sleep Time / Time in Bed) × 100
```

**Example:**
- Time in bed: 8 hours (480 minutes)
- Total sleep time: 5.5 hours (330 minutes)
- Sleep efficiency: (330 / 480) × 100 = **68.75%**

**Interpretation:**
- ≥85% = good sleep efficiency; consider extending the sleep window
- 80–84% = acceptable; maintain current window
- <80% = poor; hold or slightly restrict the window further

### 3. Stimulus Control
Stimulus control re-associates the bed with sleep rather than wakefulness, worry, or screen time.

**Rules:**
- Use the bed only for sleep and sex — no reading, working, watching TV, or scrolling.
- Go to bed only when sleepy (not just tired).
- If you don't fall asleep within 20 minutes, get out of bed. Do a quiet, non-stimulating activity in dim light (reading a paper book, light stretching). Return to bed only when sleepy.
- Repeat as needed throughout the night.
- Maintain a consistent wake time every day, including weekends.

The 20-minute rule is approximate — the goal is not to watch the clock but to leave the bed before frustration and wakefulness become strongly conditioned.

### 4. Cognitive Restructuring for Sleep
Common sleep-disrupting thoughts and CBT responses:

| Thought | Balanced alternative |
|---------|---------------------|
| "I need 8 hours or I can't function." | "Sleep need varies. Most adults function adequately on 6–7 hours. One poor night rarely impairs performance as much as I expect." |
| "If I can't sleep now, tomorrow will be ruined." | "I've had poor nights before and managed. Anxiety about sleep makes it harder, not easier." |
| "I've been awake 2 hours — I'll never fall asleep." | "Sleep pressure increases with time awake. The longer I stay calm, the more likely sleep will come." |
| "My mind races and I can't stop it." | "Trying to stop thoughts amplifies them. I can let thoughts pass without engaging with them." |

### 5. Sleep Hygiene Guidelines
Sleep hygiene alone is insufficient to treat chronic insomnia, but it supports the CBT-I interventions.

**Environment:**
- Keep the bedroom cool (65–68°F / 18–20°C is optimal for most adults).
- Dark: use blackout curtains or a sleep mask. Light suppresses melatonin.
- Quiet: use earplugs or white noise if needed.
- Reserve the bedroom for sleep — remove TVs, computers, and work materials.

**Pre-sleep routine (the 60-minute wind-down):**
- Dim lights 1 hour before bed. Bright light (especially blue-spectrum light from screens) suppresses melatonin onset.
- No screens 30–60 minutes before bed, or use blue-light filters.
- Avoid caffeine after 2 PM (caffeine half-life is 5–7 hours).
- Avoid alcohol within 3 hours of bed — it fragments sleep architecture and reduces REM sleep.
- Light snack if hungry (hunger can disrupt sleep); avoid heavy meals within 2 hours of bed.

**Daytime behaviors:**
- Get morning light exposure (natural daylight) within 30 minutes of waking — this anchors the circadian rhythm.
- Exercise regularly; avoid vigorous exercise within 2 hours of bedtime.
- Limit naps to 20 minutes before 3 PM. Long or late naps reduce sleep pressure.

## Sleep Diary Template
Track daily for CBT-I:

| Field | What to record |
|-------|---------------|
| Bedtime | Time you got into bed |
| Sleep onset | Estimated time you fell asleep |
| Number of awakenings | How many times you woke |
| Wake time | Time of final awakening |
| Get-up time | Time you got out of bed |
| Total sleep time | Estimate of total minutes asleep |
| Sleep quality | Rating 1 (very poor) – 5 (excellent) |
| Notes | Alcohol, caffeine, stress, illness |
