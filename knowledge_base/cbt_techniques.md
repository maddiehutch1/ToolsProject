# Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) Techniques

## What Is CBT?
Cognitive Behavioral Therapy is a structured, evidence-based form of psychotherapy that focuses on the relationship between thoughts, feelings, and behaviors. The core premise is that distorted or unhelpful thought patterns contribute to emotional distress and maladaptive behaviors — and that changing those patterns leads to relief.

## Thought Records
A thought record is a structured worksheet used to identify and challenge automatic negative thoughts (ANTs).

**Steps:**
1. **Identify the situation** — What happened? Where were you, and who was involved?
2. **Notice the emotion** — What did you feel? Rate intensity 0–100%.
3. **Identify the automatic thought** — What ran through your mind automatically?
4. **Examine the evidence** — What evidence supports this thought? What evidence contradicts it?
5. **Generate a balanced thought** — What is a more realistic, balanced interpretation?
6. **Re-rate the emotion** — After the balanced thought, how intense is the feeling now?

**Example:**
- Situation: Made an error in a work presentation.
- Automatic thought: "I'm completely incompetent and will be fired."
- Evidence for: Made one mistake.
- Evidence against: Received positive feedback on other slides; colleagues make mistakes too.
- Balanced thought: "I made one mistake. That doesn't define my overall performance."

## Cognitive Distortions
Cognitive distortions are systematic errors in thinking that fuel anxiety and depression. Recognizing them is the first step to challenging them.

| Distortion | Description | Example |
|------------|-------------|---------|
| All-or-nothing thinking | Seeing things in black and white, no middle ground | "If I'm not perfect, I'm a total failure." |
| Catastrophizing | Expecting the worst possible outcome | "I felt anxious at the meeting — I'll have a breakdown." |
| Mind reading | Assuming you know what others are thinking | "They didn't reply — they must hate me." |
| Overgeneralization | Drawing broad conclusions from one event | "I failed once, so I always fail." |
| Emotional reasoning | Treating feelings as facts | "I feel worthless, therefore I am worthless." |
| Should statements | Rigid internal rules that create guilt | "I should never feel angry." |
| Personalization | Blaming yourself for things outside your control | "My friend is upset — it must be something I did." |
| Filtering | Focusing exclusively on negatives while ignoring positives | Remembering only the one critical comment out of ten compliments. |

## Behavioral Activation
Depression often leads to withdrawal from pleasurable or meaningful activities, which deepens the depression. Behavioral activation interrupts this cycle by scheduling activities that produce a sense of accomplishment or pleasure — even when motivation is absent.

**Steps:**
1. Monitor daily activities and mood for one week to identify patterns.
2. List activities that historically brought pleasure, connection, or a sense of mastery.
3. Schedule 1–3 small, achievable activities per day. Start small.
4. Complete the activity regardless of motivation level ("act first, feel later").
5. Track mood before and after — this builds awareness of the activity–mood link.

**Activity categories to consider:**
- Physical: short walk, stretching, dancing to one song
- Social: texting a friend, calling a family member
- Creative: drawing, cooking a new recipe, journaling
- Mastery: completing a small task, organizing a drawer

## Cognitive Restructuring
Cognitive restructuring is the broader process of identifying, challenging, and replacing distorted thoughts. It combines thought records with Socratic questioning.

**Key Socratic questions:**
- What is the evidence for and against this thought?
- Am I confusing a thought with a fact?
- What would I tell a friend who had this thought?
- What is the most realistic outcome?
- Even if the worst happens, could I cope?

## Exposure Therapy (Core CBT Component)
For anxiety, CBT uses gradual exposure — facing feared situations in a controlled, systematic way to extinguish the fear response.

1. Build a fear hierarchy (rank situations from least to most anxiety-provoking).
2. Begin with the least anxiety-provoking situation.
3. Remain in the situation until anxiety reduces by at least 50% (habituation).
4. Repeat exposures until that step causes minimal anxiety, then move to the next.

Avoidance maintains anxiety; approach and persistence reduce it.
